Help for Rocket/Asteroid Applet -- Chris Dolan, UWisc Astronomy, 1998

You can use this program to watch a realistic animation of our solar system.
Click the "Run" button to start or stop the simulation
Click the "Restart" button put the planets back where they started.
Click the "Options" button to change the orbit of the Rocket or Asteroid.
You can increase or decrease the speed of the animation by changing the time step.
You can zoom in or zoom out to see different views of the solar system.
By default, you are looking down at the solar system centered on the Sun, but you can change which body to center on with the pull-down menu.
If you click "Draw Trails" the planets will leave dots along their paths so you can trace the orbits.

--- Options Page ---

Rocket Mode:
On the Options screen, you can adjust when the Rocket blasts off from the Earth as well as how fast and in which direction it goes. For the sake of simplicity, the launch velocity is a simple speed relative to the Earth, ignoring the complication of the Earth's gravity.
If you turn on "Capture Physics" at the bottom of the page, then if the Rocket gets within 3 million km of a planet, it will land.
If the Rocket successfully lands on another planet, it will wait a certain amount of time (specified on the Options screen) and then take off again to try to get back to Earth, based on the direction and velocity you enter.
The "Destination" is used to tell the program where you are trying to send the Rocket. You can actually land on any planet even if it is not your stated destination, but the program will only report by how much you missed a planet if it is your destination.
The default scenario is that the Rocket and the planets all move in a two-dimensional plane (ignoring distance in and out of the screen) for simplicity. If you turn off "Use 2D physics" then the full-blown 3D motions of the planets will be used. In this case, the screen is in the plane of the Earth's orbit. In 3D physics it is nearly impossible to simulate a gravity assist off another planet, since there is no way to specify the Rocket's inclination from the Ecliptic plane.
Asteroid Mode:
On the Options screen, you can enter parameters to specify where the asteroid is when you first look at it. You specify
(1) how far it is from the Earth, which you can deduce via parallax measurements;
(2) what's the angle from the Sun, which you can deduce from the coordinates of the asteroid and the time of the observations;
(3) the tangential velocity of the asteroid, which you can deduce from the proper motion of the asteroid, along with its distance from the Earth;
(4) the radial velocity of the asteroid, which you can measure by observing a spectrum of the asteroid.
If you turn on "Capture Physics" at the bottom of the page, then if the Asteroid gets within 400,000 km of a planet, it will collide. Of course, in reality it has to get much closer than this, but we thought we'd make this a little more interesting.
The default scenario is that the Asteroid and the planets all move in 3D space. If you turn on "2D physics" then the program ignores the motions and distance in and out of the screen. This makes it much more likely that you will see collisions or gravity slingshots in the motion of the asteroid. In 3D space, these encounters are extremely rare, since the orbital plane of the Asteroid is inclined to the orbits of all the planets except the Earth.


--- About this program ---
This program is a realistic simulation of the major bodies in our solar system.
It uses only Newton's Law of gravity (F=GMm/r^2) to simulate the motions of the planets.
The technique to translate forces into an animation is called an "N Body Algorithm" where in this case, N=11 (the Sun, nine planets and a Rocket or Asteroid).
Specifically, it is an adaptive step-size, Runga-Kutta integrator algorithm
Even the Sun moves due to the tug of the planets, but its motion is very small since its mass is so large.
As implied above, this program is multi-purpose. Depending on the initial parameters, this acts as either a Rocket simulator for the Astro 114 Astronautics lab or as an Asteroid simulator for the Astro 114 Doomsday Asteroid lab. The key difference is the way the Options page behaves.
